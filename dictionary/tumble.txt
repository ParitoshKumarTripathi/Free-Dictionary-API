TUMBLE
Tum"ble, v. i. [imp. & p. p. Tumbled; p. pr. & vb. n. Tumbling.]
Etym: [OE. tumblen, AS. tumbian to turn heels over head, to dance
violently; akin to D. tuimelen to fall, Sw. tumla, Dan. tumle, Icel.
tumba; and cf. G. taumeln to reel, to stagger.]

1. To roll over, or to and fro; to throw one's self about; as, a
person on pain tumbles and tosses.

2. To roll down; to fall suddenly and violently; to be precipitated;
as, to tumble from a scaffold.
He who tumbles from a tower surely has a greater blow than he who
slides from a molehill. South.

3. To play tricks by various movements and contortions of the body;
to perform the feats of an acrobat. Rowe. To tumble home (Naut.), to
incline inward, as the sides of a vessel, above the bends or extreme
breadth; -- used esp. in the phrase tumbling home. Cf. Wall-sided.
TUMBLE

=====
Tum"ble, v. t.

1. To turn over; to turn or throw about, as for examination or
search; to roll or move in a rough, coarse, or unceremonious manner;
to throw down or headlong; to precipitate; -- sometimes with over,
about, etc.; as, to tumble books or papers.

2. To disturb; to rumple; as, to tumble a bed.
TUMBLE

=====
Tum"ble, n.

Defn: Act of tumbling, or rolling over; a fall.
